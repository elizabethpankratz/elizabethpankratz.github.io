Build intuitions

Why do we care about power?

Short answer: power analyses are useful because they tell us how many experimental participants we should try to recruit.

What is statistical power?

In the world of null hypothesis significance testing (NHST), a statistical test can lead to one of two possible decisions:

  1. You reject the null hypothesis (H0).
  2. You fail to reject the H0.

Ideally, these decisions would map directly to two different true states of the world:

  1. In reality, the H0 is false.
  2. In reality, the H0 is true.

However, even if the H0 is really truly false, we are (alas!) not guaranteed to reject it.

Every test has some probability of correctly rejecting the H0 (that is, rejecting the H0 when it really truly is false). That probability is known as “statistical power”.

TODO collapsed callout: classic 2x2 table

Statistical power depends on three values:

  1. our accepted false positive rate (aka our alpha level, aka the probability of incorrectly rejecting the H0);
  2. the true size of the effect out there in reality; and
  3. the number of observations we have gathered.

In Psychology and sister disciplines, we usually set our accepted false positive rate (1) at a 5% level of risk.

Re (2), we don’t know the true effect size (that’s why we are doing stats about it!). But we can use our expert judgements and previous research make some educated guesses about what kinds of values are reasonable and/or theoretically interesting.

Finally, we’ll simulate a bunch of different sample sizes (3), so that we can see how many participants we need for a reasonably-powered study.

Simulation, you say?

Power analysis for experimental data should happen after piloting the experiment and before running the main thing (hence “a priori power analysis”).

Why run the pilot first? The pilot data helps us approximate how much variability we should expect from where, e.g.,

  • how much by-participant variability?
  • how much by-stimulus variability?
  • how much residual variability?

1: Start simple(ish)

Imagine we’re running a lexical decision task to analyse the effect of a word’s frequency on how quickly people identify it as a word in their language.

Our outcome variable will be logged reaction times (RT), because many of you said you work with continuous outcomes, and today will be more pleasant if we spare ourselves the extra spice of the link function. (But the same principles apply!)

For our predictor, because many of you said you work with categorical predictors, words will be grouped into two frequency classes: one for low-frequency words, one for high-frequency words. (But Section 2 below LINK TODO will use a continuous z-scored predictor!)

Our variables for this analysis will be the following:

  • logRT: logged reaction times (smaller values = faster)
  • wdFreq: categorical, with two levels: low, high; within-participants
  • ppt_id: categorical, one value per participant

Build up one iteration of the power analysis, step by step

Work out the maximal model

Here’s the model formula in R syntax:

logRT ~ wdFreq + (wdFreq | ppt_id)

This formula doesn’t tell us much about what parameters the model will actually contain.

If you want to see a mathematical version of the model specification where all the parameters are laid out explicitly, then you can uncollapse the box below.

TipFor the keen: Mathematical model specification

\[ \begin{align} \text{logRT}_i &= (\beta_0 + u_{0i}) + (\beta_1 + u_{1i}) \cdot \text{wdFreq} + \epsilon \\ \epsilon &\sim \text{Normal}(0, \sigma) \\ \pmatrix{u_{0i} \\ u_{1i}} &\sim \text{Normal} \begin{bmatrix} \pmatrix{0 \\ 0}, \pmatrix{\sigma_{u_{0i}}^2 & 0 \\ 0 & \sigma_{u_{1i}}^2} \end{bmatrix} \\ \end{align} \]

In words:

  • Each participant \(i\)’s log RT equals
    • the intercept \(\beta_0\) plus each participant’s intercept adjustment \(u_{0i}\); plus
    • the slope over wdFreq \(\beta_1\) plus each participant’s slope adjustment \(u_{1i}\), all times wdFreq; plus
    • some error \(\epsilon\).
  • The error \(\epsilon\) is sampled from a Normal distribution with mean 0 and standard deviation (SD) \(\sigma\).
  • Each participant’s adjustments to intercept \(u_{0i}\) and slope \(u_{1i}\) are sampled together from a bivariate Normal distribution.
    • The mean of each dimension is 0.
    • The spread of the distribution is defined by a variance-covariance matrix with the variance (SD\(^2\)) of each adjustment on the diagonal and the correlations between those adjustments on the off-diagonal. Here the correlations are assumed to be zero.
    • NB: Because slope and intercept adjustments are uncorrelated in this example, I could also have written them being drawn from two separate univariate Normals, like in the \(\epsilon\) line. However, the simr code below wants a variance-covariance matrix, even if the correlations are 0, so for consistency, I’m using the bivariate Normal version here.

Parameter glossary:

  • \(i\) indexes participants
  • \(\beta_0\) = fixed intercept
  • \(u_{0i}\) = by-participant adjustment to fixed intercept
  • \(\beta_1\) = fixed slope over wdFreq
  • \(u_{1i}\) = by-participant adjustment to fixed slope
  • \(\epsilon\) = error
  • \(\sigma\) = standard deviation of errors
  • \(\sigma_{0i}\) = SD of by-participant intercept adjustment
  • \(\sigma_{1i}\) = SD of by-participant slope adjustment

Define how all variables are coded/transformed

We already know that logRT is in log units, not ms.

wdFreq will be sum-coded, aka effect-coded, aka deviation-coded, aka (because names for contrast coding schemes are basically meaningless these days): low will be coded as –0.5, and high as +0.5.

Consequently, the intercept \(\beta_0\) will represent the grand mean log RT, and the slope \(\beta_1\) will represent the change in log RT as we move from low to high.
