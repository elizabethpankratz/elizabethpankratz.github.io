Research in academia

For understanding people and the world

Academic research


Employers: Universities, research institutes

Purpose: Create generalisable knowledge/theory

Data and methods: Bespoke datasets, rigorous controlled experiments, transparent and reproducible analyses (based on principles of Open Science)

Time scales: Months, years

Output and success metrics: Peer-reviewed journal papers,
talks at conferences

How do I pursue a career in academia?


Pursue postgraduate studies (MSc, PhD)

  • Work closely with supervisor(s) to develop area of expertise and conduct original research

Temporary work as a post-doc

  • Do research on existing projects
  • Apply for your own grants to fund your work

(Ideally) gain a permanent research position at a university

  • e.g., as lecturer (UK) / assistant professor (elsewhere)
  • Very competitive: there are way more amazingly qualified people than there are jobs

The wide world outside of academia


At uni, your main role models will probably be people who’ve pursued an academic career.

All of those people have gotten extremely lucky to be where we are.

And the academic landscape is changing quickly,
so what current staff went through is different from what you will go through.


You have many other options open to you!

Research in the public sector

1/3: For supporting social good

Supporting social good: Charities/NGOs


Employers: Charity organisations, social enterprises/foundations,
non-governmental organisations (NGOs)

Purpose: Support vulnerable groups, design and improve charity programmes

Data and methods: Mixed methods: surveys, interviews, focus groups, basic quant analysis skills (e.g., descriptive stats)

Time scales: Rapid deadlines for immediate needs, especially in crises

Output and success metrics: Reports that pair data with human stories, amount of funding acquired

Research in the public sector

2/3: For improving public health

Improving public health: Health organisations


Employers: NHS Scotland boards (e.g., NHS Lothian Research & Development Offices), health charities

Purpose: Inform actions and decisions to protect people’s health

Data and methods: Routine/admin/surveillance data,
quasi-experimental data, natural experiments, meta-analyses

Time scales: Variable, based around operational/urgent deadlines

Output and success metrics: Briefings, guidance, reports, advice for health care providers

Research in the public sector

3/3: For affecting public policy

Affecting public policy: Government


Employers: Scottish government (e.g., Scottish Government Social Research)

Purpose: Make sure policy decisions are fair and cost-effective

Data and methods: Mixed methods: surveys, qualitative fieldwork, quasi-experimental data, natural experiments, meta-analyses; increasing focus on reproducibility/Open Science

Time scales: Days/weeks/months, based around fiscal cycles and ministerial deadlines

Output and success metrics: Briefings, guidance, reports, advice for government agencies

Research in the private sector

1/2: For making profitable decisions

Making profitable decisions: Market/consumer insights


Employers: Companies, consultancies, commercial organisations

Purpose: Match commercial decisions to what consumers want

Data and methods: Mixed methods: Surveys, focus groups, A/B tests, basic quant analysis skills (e.g., descriptive stats)

Time scales: Days/weeks/months, iterative sprints

Output and success metrics: Slides, reports, dashboards; results are proprietary/secret

Research in the private sector

2/2: For enhancing user experience

Enhancing user experience: UX/service design


Employers: Companies, consultancies, commercial organisations

Purpose: Inform product/service decisions, often using psychological concepts

Data and methods: Mixed methods: Small targeted samples, basic quant analysis skills (e.g., descriptive stats)

Time scales: Days/weeks/months, iterative sprints

Output and success metrics: Usability findings, design recommendations, prototypes; results are proprietary/secret

The “how”

How do I pursue a career outside of academia?


Attend career fairs/events

  • Talk to people who have more experience than we do!

Try out internships

  • Test the waters: see if you actually like a particular kind of job
  • Get people who are already established on your side

Use the available resources

  • UoE’s Careers Service
  • UK Government’s National Careers Service website
  • Skills Development Scotland’s My World of Work website

The big picture

Academic vs. non-academic research


Similarities across all kinds of research:

  • Ethical considerations apply everywhere (e.g., GDPR).
  • All analyses should be thoughtful and principled.
  • Communicating results clearly is a key part of the research cycle.

Key differences outside of academia:

  • The pace of work is much quicker.
  • “Good enough” samples and evidence are the norm.
  • Your work directly impacts people/policy/decision-making.

Some parting advice


Instead of thinking about what job title you want, think about:

  • how would you like to work?
    • “I want to use my coding skills”
    • “I want to be able to work remotely”
  • what are your values for your work?
    • “I want my work to have a real-world impact”
    • “I want to make a lot of money”

Looking for jobs that fit your preferences and values, instead of looking for jobs with a particular title, can open up all kinds of interesting options.
